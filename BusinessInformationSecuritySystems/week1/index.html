Good Tech Means More Risk

  • Standalone systems evolving into networked systems.
  • Networked systems leading to the internet/www, with more diverse and connected users.
  • The transition from the internet to mobile computing: Is anyone left who isn’t connected?
  • The next stages: Cloud computing, the Metaverse, Machine learning, and potentially true AI.

Drivers of Connectivity

  • Instant communication: The decreasing willingness to wait for a response.
  • Access to new target groups: Exploring unexploited groups as regional markets become saturated.
  • Data sharing: The impact of rapid access to up-to-date and accurate data on decision-making effectiveness.

Possible BISS Solutions

  • Understand the security threats by drawing on the right knowledge sources.
  • Use risk assessment to target security investments in the most beneficial areas.
  • Integrate security into normal business behavior through clear policy and staff education.
  • Deploy integrated technical controls and keep them up to date.
  • Respond quickly and effectively to breaches, e.g., by planning ahead for contingencies.

BISS Solutions Approach

We tend to use a 3-pronged approach:

  • Prevention
  • Detection
  • Response

This is not a final solution, but a cycle of continuous improvement.

BISS Fundamentals

Systems

System: a set of functional components, either people or digital technologies, working together to achieve a specific purpose.

Socio-technical System: systems that have interactions between people and technologies in a given operating context, without direct interaction with the physical environment (a website).

Cyber-Physical-Social System: systems that combine digital (cyber) technologies and people (social) with interactions with their physical operating environment (remote access to a 3D printer).

As systems evolve so do the risks that come with them.

Information and Cyber Security

Data: Output from any form of device (24).

Information: Data that has been given processed and given meaning (24kg).

Information Security: This is about protecting all information against unauthorized access that could result in undesired data modification or theft.

Cyber Security: Cybersecurity refers to the practice of protecting data, its related technologies, and storage sources from threats.

The more your organisation focuses on information, the more important IS becomes, and in some cases, it almost subsumes Cyber Security.

Information Security Triad

Confidentiality: The property that information is only made available or disclosed to authorized individuals, entities, or processes.

Integrity: The property of safeguarding the accuracy and completeness of assets against unauthorized access or modification.

Availability: The property of being accessible and usable on demand by an authorized entity.

Access Controls

Authentication: Links an identity to an entity. A single entity can have many identities, such as a user owning multiple accounts on a single computer.

Authorisation: The process by which an entity is permitted to perform certain activities on a system’s resources.

Accounting: Collects and analyses data about activity within a system. It is usually performed to uncover security weaknesses (this is sometimes called accountability).

Risk Analysis

Assets: Anything that has value to an organisation, including people and concepts. (ISO/IEC 13335)

Threat: A potential cause of an unwanted incident, which may result in harm to a system or organisation. (Rainclouds) (ISO/IEC 27000)

Vulnerability: A weakness of an asset or control that can be exploited by one or more threats. (Not taking an umbrella) (ISO/IEC 27000)

Risk: The combination of the Threat and Vulnerability. (The risk of it raining and you getting wet) (ISO/IEC 27000)

Impact: The result of an information security incident, caused by a threat, which affects assets. (ISO/IEC 13335)

Control: Any administrative, managerial, technical, or legal method that can be used to mitigate risk. (ISO/IEC 27000)

Key Steps in Risk Analysis

  • Classification of data assets according to their sensitivity/value.
  • Identification of threats and vulnerabilities.
  • Likelihood occurrences and impact estimation of these threats.
  • Implementation of security controls at an acceptable cost.

Approaches to Risk Analysis

Risk analysis should be a continuous process to monitor the effectiveness of implemented security controls as well as to develop proactive and resilient security capabilities. Approaches include Quantitative, Heuristic, Systems, Mixed approaches, and various techniques, tools, and services.

A Quantitative Approach to Risk Analysis

Create your risk exposure by listing all likely risk areas, items/events, probabilities, outcomes, and calculating the exposure of risk (usually probability * likely cost).

From Risk Analysis to Risk Management

Decision making in risk management involves the following strategies:

  • Avoidance: Removing the source of the risk or adjusting plans to prevent the risk.
  • Transfer: Moving the liability for the risk to a third party, like insurance coverage.
  • Mitigate: Reducing the probability or impact of the risk.
  • Accept: Choosing not to act against the risk and developing a workaround if it occurs.
  • Increase: Taking on more risk to pursue an opportunity.

Heuristic Approach to Risk Analysis

“Strategies that attempt to assess risk based on experience and partial information using rules of thumb are known as ‘heuristic’” (Curtis G., 1995)

A commonly used heuristic method is a checklist, developed from previous experiences and lessons learned, to review systems or controls.

Risk Assessment Standards

Key standards include:

  • NIST SP800-30 Guide for Conducting Risk Assessments: csrc.nist.gov
  • ISO 27005, Information Security Risk Management: itgovernance.co.uk
