Week 4: Social Engineering

Social Engineering: Basic Concept

Social engineering involves the art of convincing individuals to disclose sensitive, restricted, or confidential information. It often includes manipulation tactics that lead to actions like disclosing passwords or installing malware through deceptive means.

Examples of Social Engineering Techniques

  • Spear Phishing: Targeting a specific company.
  • Net Phishing: Targeting a group of people or random individuals.
  • Pretexting: Fabricating a scenario to steal personal information.
  • Blackmail/Intimidation: Using information or force to compel victims.
  • Baiting: Tricking or enticing victims into revealing information.
  • Tailgating/Piggybacking: Unauthorized access by following an authorized individual.
  • Vishing: Using phone calls to scam victims into divulging information.
  • Smishing: Text messages to lure victims into clicking malicious links or providing personal information.
  • Impersonation: Pretending to be a trusted contact or authority.

Intelligent Password Guessing

Intelligent password guessing involves information gathering and deception techniques to gain unauthorized access.

Information Gathering

Examples include obtaining dates of birth, important dates, family birthdays for pin codes, compiling wordlists from websites and social media, and using active information gathering methods.

Disadvantages of Dictionary Attacks

  • Uncertain outcomes.
  • Detection and blacklisting risks.
  • Honeypot vulnerabilities.
  • Intelligent guessing involving default passwords, etc.

Deception

  • Honeypots: Simulated systems to deceive attackers.
  • Honey Users: Fake user accounts for detection.
  • Honey Tokens: Fake credentials to attract attackers.
  • Honey Nets: Networks designed to lure and detect attackers.

Obfuscation

Switching port numbers, plugin names, directory names, etc., to confuse attackers.

Why Are We Targeted?

  • Trust: Attackers exploit trust in individuals and systems.
  • Lack of Proper Training: Many companies don't adequately train or re-train employees.
  • Readily Available Information: Information and assets are easily accessible.
  • Disparate Units/Sites: Larger companies may have differing policies across units.
  • Lack of Policies: Many organizations still lack proper security policies.
  • Awareness: Employee awareness of existing policies is often insufficient.
