Week 4: Cryptosystems and Block Ciphers

Cryptosystem

A cryptosystem consists of a 5-tuple (E, D, M, K, C) such that:

  • M, K, and C are finite sets
  • M is the set of plaintexts
  • K is the set of keys
  • C is the set of ciphertexts
  • E is the encryption algorithm sets E : M × K ⇒ C
  • D is the decryption algorithm sets D : C × K ⇒ M

Towards Principles of Modern Cryptography

Ciphers considered so far were:

  1. Vulnerable to exhaustive search
  2. Vulnerable to frequency analysis
  3. Small change in plaintext results in small change in ciphertext
  4. Insufficient to rely on secrecy of the algorithm used

Modern algorithms abide by the principles of:

  1. Large enough key space to resist exhaustive search
  2. Resistant to frequency analysis
  3. Small change in plaintext results in a large change in ciphertext
  4. Security depends only on secrecy of the key, not on secrecy of the algorithm (Kerckhoff’s principle)

Substitution Permutation Networks

Substitution Permutation Networks (SPN) are a fundamental structure in modern block ciphers. They provide a systematic approach to designing secure symmetric-key encryption algorithms. In SPN, the encryption process involves the repeated application of two main operations: substitution and permutation.

Substitution involves replacing elements of the plaintext with other elements, typically through the use of S-boxes (substitution boxes). Permutation, on the other hand, involves rearranging the order of the elements. The combination of these operations enhances the confusion and diffusion properties of the encryption, contributing to its security.

Logical Operations

Logical operations play a crucial role in the design of cryptographic algorithms. These operations include bitwise operations like AND, OR, XOR (exclusive OR), and NOT. Logical operations are fundamental in manipulating individual bits and creating complex transformations within ciphers.

In block ciphers, logical operations are often employed to achieve confusion and diffusion, ensuring that the relationship between the plaintext and ciphertext is not easily discernible. XOR, in particular, is a common operation used for its simplicity and effectiveness in introducing diffusion.

XOR Cipher

The XOR (exclusive OR) cipher is a simple yet powerful encryption technique. It involves applying the XOR operation between each bit of the plaintext and a corresponding bit of the key. XOR has the property that applying the operation twice with the same key results in the original value.

While XOR alone may not be suitable for strong encryption, it serves as a fundamental building block in various cryptographic algorithms. In particular, XOR is often used in combination with other operations within block ciphers and stream ciphers to achieve the desired security properties.

Substitution and Permutation Recap

Substitution and permutation are core concepts in modern cryptography, often used in the design of block ciphers. Substitution involves replacing elements of the plaintext with others, typically through S-boxes, while permutation involves rearranging the order of the elements.

These operations contribute to the confusion and diffusion properties of a cipher, making it resistant to various attacks. By carefully combining substitution and permutation, cryptographic algorithms aim to achieve a balance between efficiency and security.

Heys Cipher

The Heys Cipher is a symmetric-key block cipher known for its simplicity and efficiency. It follows a Feistel network structure, a common design approach in block ciphers. The Feistel network involves dividing the plaintext into two halves, applying different functions to each half in multiple rounds, and combining the results.

Heys Cipher employs S-boxes and permutation layers within each round, contributing to its security. While not as widely adopted as some other ciphers, Heys Cipher illustrates the diversity of design choices in the field of cryptography.

DES History

The Data Encryption Standard (DES) is a historic symmetric-key algorithm widely used in the late 20th century. Developed in the early 1970s by IBM and adopted as a federal standard, DES played a crucial role in securing sensitive information for many years.

DES operates on 64-bit blocks of data using a 56-bit key. Over time, as computational power increased, DES faced vulnerabilities due to its relatively short key length. This led to the development of Triple DES (3DES) and eventually the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) to address emerging security concerns.
